The current SVAR approach fails to capture the "Lock-in Effect" (or "Rate-Lock"), where existing homeowners are discouraged from selling due to the gap between their current low-interest mortgage and prevailing high market rates. This phenomenon creates a non-linear feedback loop where rising mortgage rates simultaneously suppress new housing starts (via financing costs) and restrict existing supply (via the lock-in effect), causing home prices to remain sticky or rise despite cooling demand. 

I hypothesize that the "Supply Shock" identified in previous iterations is conflated because it treats supply as purely construction-based. By constructing a "Rate-Spread" variable—defined as the difference between the current `MORTGAGE30US` and the 5-year rolling average of mortgage rates—we can model the "Lock-in" intensity. I propose a Threshold Vector Autoregression (TVAR) model that uses this Rate-Spread as the threshold variable. This will allow the model to switch between two regimes: a "Low-Spread" regime (market-clearing) and a "High-Spread" regime (supply-constrained/lock-in). This approach will test whether the transmission of interest rate shocks to home prices becomes significantly more inelastic (or even positive) when the Rate-Spread exceeds a critical threshold, thereby explaining the residual non-Gaussian behavior and the sluggish price response observed in previous iterations.
