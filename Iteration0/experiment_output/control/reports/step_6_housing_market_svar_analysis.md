### 1. Data Preprocessing and Stationarity Analysis

This section presents the empirical findings from the Structural Vector Autoregression (SVAR) model designed to decompose the volatility of the U.S. housing market into exogenous demand, supply, and policy shocks. The analysis spans the period from January 1987 to January 2024.

The raw data series—S&P/Case-Shiller U.S. National Home Price Index (`CSUSHPINSA`), Housing Starts (`HOUST`), and the 30-Year Fixed Rate Mortgage Average (`MORTGAGE30US`)—were harmonized to a monthly frequency, yielding a sample of 445 observations. To ensure that the structural shocks do not spuriously capture seasonal lending or construction patterns, a Seasonal and Trend decomposition using Loess (STL) was applied to all series. The diagnostic plots of the raw versus seasonally adjusted series confirm the successful removal of residual seasonality, providing a clean dataset for the subsequent time-series analysis.

Before specifying the VAR model, rigorous stationarity and structural break tests were conducted to determine the appropriate order of integration and to identify potential regime shifts. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) and Phillips-Perron (PP) tests were employed on both the levels and first differences of the series. For the Case-Shiller Index, the ADF test on the level yielded a test statistic of 1.9762 ($p = 0.9986$), while the first difference was stationary with a statistic of -2.9579 ($p = 0.0390$). Similarly, the PP test confirmed non-stationarity in levels ($p = 0.9988$) and stationarity in differences ($p = 0.0178$). Housing Starts exhibited similar $I(1)$ behavior, with the ADF test on the level failing to reject the null hypothesis of a unit root ($p = 0.1806$) and the first difference showing stationarity ($p = 0.0204$). The Mortgage Rate series was also found to be $I(1)$, with the ADF test on the first difference yielding a highly significant statistic of -5.7729 ($p < 0.001$).

Given that all three series are integrated of order one, $I(1)$, the Johansen Cointegration Test was performed to investigate the presence of long-run equilibrium relationships. The Trace Statistic for the null hypothesis of no cointegration ($r \le 0$) was 26.4184, which is below the 95% critical value of 29.7961. The Maximum Eigenvalue Statistic for $r \le 0$ was 18.9592, also falling short of the 95% critical value of 21.1314. Consequently, the null hypothesis of no cointegration could not be rejected at the 5% significance level. This finding dictates that a Vector Error Correction Model (VECM) is inappropriate, and the VAR model should be specified in first differences to ensure stationarity without losing structural information.

To account for potential regime shifts that could distort the VAR estimates, the Zivot-Andrews structural break test was conducted, allowing for a single break in the intercept. The test identified a highly significant structural break in Housing Starts on February 2006 (Test Statistic = -5.7781, $p = 0.0014$), perfectly aligning with the peak and subsequent collapse of the mid-2000s housing construction boom. Conversely, the Case-Shiller Index and Mortgage Rates did not exhibit statistically significant structural breaks at the 5% level, with identified potential break dates in March 2018 ($p = 0.9990$) and November 2017 ($p = 0.9977$), respectively. The Zivot-Andrews test statistics over time are visually documented in the generated diagnostic plots, highlighting the sharp deviation in construction activity leading up to the 2008 financial crisis.

### 2. VAR Model Specification and Diagnostics

Based on the stationarity results, the endogenous vector $Y_t$ was defined as $[\Delta(\text{MORTGAGE30US}), \Delta \log(\text{HOUST}), \Delta \log(\text{CSUSHPINSA})]$. A block-exogenous structure was imposed on the mortgage rate equation, reflecting the assumption that while mortgage rates influence housing market dynamics, they are primarily driven by broader macroeconomic factors and monetary policy, rather than contemporaneous idiosyncratic shocks in housing starts or prices.

The optimal lag length for the VAR model was determined using standard information criteria. The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) selected a lag order of $p = 4$, which was adopted to capture the delayed transmission mechanisms inherent in real estate markets, such as construction lead times and mortgage approval processes. The estimated block-exogenous coefficients for the mortgage rate equation reveal significant autoregressive dynamics, with the first lag exhibiting a coefficient of 0.4202, followed by -0.1654 at the second lag, 0.1295 at the third lag, and -0.0502 at the fourth lag.

Diagnostic checks on the estimated VAR residuals were performed to assess model adequacy. The multivariate Portmanteau test for autocorrelation up to 16 lags yielded a test statistic of 198.5125 ($p = 3 \times 10^{-6}$), indicating some degree of residual autocorrelation. Furthermore, the multivariate Jarque-Bera test strongly rejected the null hypothesis of normality (Test Statistic = 552.5961, $p < 0.001$). While these diagnostic results suggest deviations from the classical Gaussian white-noise assumptions—a common feature in high-frequency macro-financial data characterized by fat tails and volatility clustering—the structural identification via sign restrictions relies on the median responses from a robust sampling algorithm, which mitigates the impact of non-normal residual distributions.

### 3. Structural Identification and Impulse Response Analysis

To isolate the latent drivers of housing market volatility, structural shocks were identified using sign restrictions on the impact matrix. The theoretical priors were defined as follows: a 'Policy Shock' (an exogenous tightening of financing costs) increases mortgage rates while decreasing both housing starts and home prices; a 'Demand Shock' increases mortgage rates, housing starts, and home prices simultaneously; and a 'Supply Shock' decreases mortgage rates, increases housing starts, and decreases home prices.

The Rubio-Ramirez et al. (2010) algorithm was employed to draw from the space of orthogonal rotation matrices. The algorithm demonstrated high efficiency, requiring only 1,740 attempts to secure 1,000 accepted draws that satisfied all sign restrictions. This acceptance rate of 57.47% provides strong empirical validation that the imposed theoretical constraints are highly consistent with the underlying data generating process of the U.S. housing market.

The accumulated Impulse Response Functions (IRFs) were computed over a 36-month horizon, with median responses and 68%/95% credible intervals extracted to quantify the magnitude and persistence of the structural shocks. The generated IRF summary plots visually confirm the expected directional movements and highlight the dynamic adjustment paths.

**Response to a Policy Shock:**
A one-standard-deviation restrictive Policy Shock leads to an immediate increase in the mortgage rate by 0.0870 percentage points (pp) at impact, which persistently builds to 0.1306 pp by month 36. In response to this tightening of financing costs, construction activity contracts sharply. Log housing starts decline by -0.0294 at impact and continue to deteriorate, reaching a cumulative decline of -0.0645 log points by the end of the three-year horizon. Home prices exhibit a more sluggish, delayed response—a hallmark of real estate market friction. The initial impact on log home prices is a negligible -0.0007, but the negative effect compounds over time, resulting in a cumulative decline of -0.0268 log points at month 36. This demonstrates that while construction intent reacts swiftly to financing costs, price discovery is a protracted process.

**Response to a Demand Shock:**
An expansionary Demand Shock triggers an immediate and sustained increase across all variables. Mortgage rates rise by 0.1179 pp at impact, stabilizing at 0.1770 pp by month 36, reflecting increased demand for credit. Housing starts jump by 0.0308 log points initially, though the momentum slightly moderates to a cumulative 0.0250 log points over the long run. Home prices respond positively, with the cumulative effect growing from 0.0006 at impact to 0.0071 log points at month 36. The persistent rise in prices despite the moderation in construction growth underscores the inelastic nature of housing supply in the face of sustained demand pressures.

**Response to a Supply Shock:**
A positive Supply Shock—characterized by an expansion in construction activity—leads to an immediate increase in log housing starts by 0.0397. However, this effect is highly transient, decaying rapidly to a mere 0.0007 log points by month 36. The influx of new housing units exerts downward pressure on prices, with log home prices declining by -0.0008 at impact and reaching a cumulative reduction of -0.0139 log points over three years. Interestingly, the supply shock is associated with a slight easing of mortgage rates (-0.0090 pp at impact, -0.0135 pp at month 36), potentially reflecting broader macroeconomic cooling associated with overbuilding.

### 4. Forecast Error Variance Decomposition (FEVD)

To quantify the relative importance of each structural shock in driving market fluctuations, a Forecast Error Variance Decomposition (FEVD) was computed at horizons of 1, 6, 12, 24, and 36 months.

For the Mortgage Rate, the variance is predominantly explained by Demand Shocks (58.56%) and Policy Shocks (31.86%), with Supply Shocks playing a minor role (9.58%). This distribution remains constant across all horizons, reflecting the block-exogenous nature of the financing cost variable in the VAR specification.

The variance in Housing Starts is heavily dominated by Supply Shocks, which account for 46.54% of the forecast error variance at the 1-month horizon and increase to 50.73% by month 36. Demand Shocks and Policy Shocks contribute roughly equally to the remaining variance, stabilizing at 25.46% and 23.81%, respectively, in the long run. This indicates that construction activity is primarily constrained and driven by supply-side factors—such as land availability, zoning regulations, and material costs—rather than purely by demand or financing conditions.

In stark contrast, the variance decomposition for Home Prices reveals a dramatic shift over time. At the 1-month horizon, the variance is relatively evenly distributed among Supply (40.86%), Policy (29.76%), and Demand (29.38%) shocks. However, as the horizon extends, the influence of Policy Shocks surges exponentially. By month 12, Policy Shocks explain 66.36% of the variance, and by month 36, they dominate price fluctuations, accounting for 70.06% of the forecast error variance. The contribution of Supply Shocks halves to 20.02%, and Demand Shocks dwindle to 9.92%. This quantitative finding is profound: while short-term price movements are noisy and driven by immediate supply-demand imbalances, the long-run trajectory of U.S. home prices is overwhelmingly dictated by the cost of financing and monetary policy regimes.

### 5. Historical Decomposition of Housing Market Dynamics

To contextualize the structural shocks within the macroeconomic timeline, a historical decomposition was performed, mapping the cumulative contributions of Policy, Demand, and Supply shocks to the observed fluctuations in the housing series. The generated historical decomposition plots provide a granular view of three pivotal market episodes: the 2000s Boom, the 2008 Bust, and the COVID-19 Surge.

**The 2000s Boom (2002–2006):**
During the mid-2000s expansion, the historical decomposition reveals that the surge in housing starts and home prices was heavily fueled by a confluence of positive Demand Shocks and accommodative Policy Shocks. The cumulative log change in home prices during this period shows a massive positive contribution from demand-side factors, reflecting the speculative frenzy and the expansion of subprime credit. Supply shocks also contributed positively to housing starts, capturing the unprecedented boom in residential construction.

**The 2008 Bust (2007–2011):**
The transition into the 2008 financial crisis is marked by a sharp reversal in the shock contributions. The structural break identified by the Zivot-Andrews test in February 2006 perfectly precedes this collapse. The historical decomposition shows that the precipitous drop in home prices and construction activity was driven by severe negative Demand Shocks, as credit markets froze and consumer confidence evaporated. Furthermore, the overhang from the previous construction boom manifested as negative Supply Shocks (in the context of excess inventory depressing the market), while restrictive Policy Shocks in the immediate lead-up to the crash exacerbated the downward pressure on prices.

**The COVID-19 Surge (2020–2022):**
The pandemic-era housing market exhibits a unique structural signature. The historical decomposition illustrates that the explosive growth in home prices was initially catalyzed by massive accommodative Policy Shocks (as the Federal Reserve slashed rates to the zero lower bound) combined with unprecedented positive Demand Shocks, driven by the shift toward remote work and the desire for more living space. Concurrently, the plot for housing starts shows significant negative Supply Shocks during 2020 and 2021, reflecting severe supply chain disruptions, labor shortages, and soaring material costs. This combination of hyper-stimulated demand and constrained supply perfectly explains the historic, vertical ascent of the Case-Shiller index during this period.

### 6. Concluding Assessment of Model Robustness and Market Dynamics

The application of a Sign-Restricted Structural VAR to the U.S. housing market has successfully disentangled the complex, endogenous relationships between financing costs, construction activity, and price discovery. The robustness of the model is evidenced by the high acceptance rate of the sign restrictions (57.47%) and the alignment of the historical decomposition with known macroeconomic events.

The quantitative results yield two critical insights into market dynamics. First, there is a pronounced asymmetry in the transmission mechanisms: construction activity (Housing Starts) reacts swiftly to shocks but is ultimately dominated by supply-side constraints (explaining 50.73% of long-run variance), whereas Home Prices exhibit sluggish short-term responses but are overwhelmingly determined by Policy Shocks in the long run (explaining 70.06% of variance at 36 months). Second, the historical decomposition confirms that while the 2008 bust was primarily a demand-side collapse exacerbated by oversupply, the COVID-19 housing boom was a dual-engine phenomenon of demand stimulus colliding with severe supply constraints. 

By circumventing the need for external macroeconomic variables and relying purely on the internal co-movement dynamics of the FRED series, this SVAR framework provides a clean, robust quantification of how interest rate volatility filters through to the real economy, offering valuable empirical evidence for both policymakers and market participants.
